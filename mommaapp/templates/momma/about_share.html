About

The idea behind Flumoo came during a postgraduate accounting class in the spring of 2015. I had been intrigued by my readings of Warren Buffett on financial analysis, securities markets, and investment strategy. Naturally, as a business student, I would find Buffet's views compelling. However, in discovering his approach to investing, I would learn something more. His meticulous dissecting of financial statements, newspaper clippings, tax documents, and other relics would serve as the foundation for which his investment empire is afixed.

Flumoo was born during a boring night lecture. My mind was wondering. I was anxious to go home. I thought it was just another class when my mind randomly latched on to something. My subconcious brain had been ruminating on a cocktail of theories and curiosities, as any curious mind does, particularly on the teachings I had been learning about in class, those of Buffett and others. As the professor announced that the class would be receiving permission to a very expensive stock-trading platform, my mind wondered how Warren Buffet would use such a tool. I wondered what he might be interested in, how he might use the computer to sort through the data. I wanted to know what kinds of questions he might have, questions about cash flow, debt positions, income statement dynamics, among others. Many years later, Flumoo seeks to realize this concept and provide theoretical utilities, predictive capabilites, and simulative overatures moving forward.

Jay Kelly, Creator

Glossary

Top To Bottom Ratio

After all is said and done, how much did it cost the firm to make its profit

Organizational Lean Ratio

How much excess is spent operating the business

Dominance Ratio

How expensive is the process of obtaining and making the firm's product or service

Behind The Scenes Ratio

How is the firm moving its non-operating cash around

Assets

Physical Cash, Checking Account, Savings Account, Certificates of Deposit, Money Market Accounts, Treasury Bills, Property/Land, Inventory, Equipment, Receivables, Goodwill, IP

Ending Cash Position

The amount of cash the firm has on hand, at that moment in time. This cash position is a sign of financial strength and liquidity of the firm, representing the ability of the firm to meet its current liabilities. Cash is the most liquid type of asset and a large portion in a valuable currency such as the US Dollar, Japanese Yen, or Swiss Franc will provide maximum utility in the marketplace. The value of currency declines as interest rates are low because access to more cash is relatively cheap. Hence, cash, the most liquid asset, is especially prized during times of high interest rates.
